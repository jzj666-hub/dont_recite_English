# 寻租

In economic terms, Rent-seeking (寻租) refers to the practice of manipulating the political or social environment to increase one's wealth without creating any new wealth in return.
While a "profit" is earned by providing a better product or service, a "rent" in this context is gained by capturing a larger slice of the existing pie—often through government intervention, lobbying, or artificial monopolies.
Here is a breakdown of the concept:
Wealth Transfer vs. Wealth Creation: Imagine a baker who makes better bread to earn money (Wealth Creation). Now imagine a baker who lobbies the government to pass a law banning all other bakeries in town (Rent-seeking). The second baker gets rich not by baking better bread, but by using "political rent" to eliminate competition.
The Mechanism: It typically involves seeking "rents" through licenses, subsidies, tariffs, or regulations. Because these rewards are granted by those in power, people spend massive amounts of resources (money, time, and effort) trying to influence officials rather than innovating.
Social Cost: Economists consider rent-seeking a "deadweight loss." The resources spent on lobbying are wasted because they don't produce anything useful for society. It often leads to corruption, market inefficiency, and increased inequality.
Connecting to our previous discussion
In the context of the political systems we were discussing, rent-seeking is often the "economic shadow" of a powerful bureaucracy. When the state has total control over resources (the "base" in Marxist terms), the temptation for individuals to engage in rent-seeking becomes very high. Instead of "revolutionizing" production, people may focus on "seeking rents" from the authorities who control the distribution of those resources.
Essentially, while a revolution aims to change who owns the pie, rent-seeking is the art of manipulating the rules to make sure you get the biggest piece without having to do the baking.
