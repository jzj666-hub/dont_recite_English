# 马克思主义

To understand how the Cultural Revolution is perceived globally and how it fits into Marxist theory, one must first recognize the dual nature of international historical discourse. From a mainstream Western perspective, the Cultural Revolution, spanning from 1966 to 1976, is predominantly characterized as a decade of profound social upheaval and "Ten Years of Chaos." Historians often focus on the humanitarian crises, the destruction of invaluable cultural heritage, and the economic stagnation resulting from radical political movements. However, a more nuanced academic view also exists, particularly among scholars of the "Global Sixties." These observers examine how the movement’s anti-establishment rhetoric resonated with student protests in Paris and civil rights activism in the United States, viewing it as a radical, albeit volatile, experiment in challenging state bureaucracy.
This historical event is deeply rooted in the Marxist definition of "revolution," which differs significantly from common political shifts. In the Marxist paradigm, a revolution is not merely a change in leadership but a fundamental transformation of both the economic "base" (the system of production) and the "superstructure" (culture, law, and ideology). According to classical Marxist theory, the development of society is driven by class struggle. A revolution occurs when the existing social relations become a hindrance to the growth of productive forces, leading the working class to overthrow the ruling elite to establish a collective ownership of resources.
The synthesis of these two concepts—the global observation of chaos and the theoretical framework of Marxism—lies in the theory of "Continuous Revolution." From a theoretical standpoint, the Cultural Revolution was an attempt to resolve a perceived contradiction: even after the economic base had been socialized, the cultural superstructure remained "contaminated" by old habits and capitalist remnants. Mao Zedong feared that without a revolution in the realm of culture and thought, the political system would inevitably revert to capitalism, a process known as revisionism. Thus, while the international community often views the era through the lens of human rights and social disruption, the internal logic of the movement was an attempt to use the superstructure to protect the socialist base. The perceived chaos was, in the eyes of its architects, a necessary "cleansing" of society to ensure ideological purity, illustrating a tragic tension between abstract political theory and the lived reality of a nation.
